TL;DR: SF-SLAM decouples rotation from the SLAM state with a drift-free San Francisco World anchor, encodes planes and slanted lines as compact 1-D coordinates, and solves everything with a single linear Kalman filter — robust across staircases, ramps, and inter-floor transitions.

Abstract

We present San Francisco SLAM (SF-SLAM), a structural RGB-D SLAM algorithm for accurate 6-DoF camera localization and mapping in geometrically complex structured environments. Most existing visual SLAM methods often fail in textureless and repetitive scenes because feature scarcity and spatial ambiguity cause cumulative trajectory drift and tracking loss. These failures become more severe in staircases and inter-floor spaces, where repeated layouts and slanted structures fall outside the assumptions of the Manhattan world.

To address these problems, we detect planes and lines to build a reliable global San Francisco world (SFW) map while simultaneously estimating 6-DoF camera poses, capturing structural regularities beyond orthogonal layouts. We decouple rotation and translation using structure-consistent absolute rotation estimates and represent structural landmarks with compact, structure-aligned parameters. This formulation streamlines state estimation and enables robust camera pose tracking and global structural mapping. Our approach jointly leverages both inter-floor planar and line regularities to preserve trajectory consistency where existing methods fail, effectively reducing accumulated drift during inter-floor transitions and outperforming state-of-the-art SLAM methods in challenging real-world environments.

Method

The San Francisco world extends the Manhattan triad with four sloping dominant directions that share a common inclination, so the entire frame is fixed by just 4 degrees of freedom. SF-SLAM exploits this structure in three stages. A graph-based rotation fusion combines locally accurate ARKit motion with a drift-free SFW Visual Compass anchor, gated online by a mutual-consensus weight so the anchor gracefully fades out when it becomes unreliable. MW-aligned planes are then refined to a single offset value via a 1-point RANSAC, while SFW-aligned lines are recovered through PCA on sampled 3-D points and classified as orthogonal or slanted. Because rotation is removed from the state and every landmark reduces to a 1-D invariant coordinate, the measurement models become strictly linear in the camera translation, yielding a single decoupled linear Kalman filter with no Jacobians and no accumulated linearization error.

SF-SLAM Dataset

Existing SLAM benchmarks do not reflect the structural characteristics required for the San Francisco world assumption, so we collected a custom dataset with an iPhone 15 Pro Max and the Stray Scanner application. It provides RGB and depth streams (1920×1440 and 256×192, at 15 Hz) together with ARKit trajectories, covering large-scale buildings and an outdoor scene. The dataset comprises four closed-loop sequences, where the camera returns to its start for a reliable drift anchor, and three open-loop stair sequences that terminate at a landmark sharing the start's planar coordinates but differing in elevation.

Results

Across closed-loop and open-loop sequences, SF-SLAM achieves the lowest final drift error against recent baselines including ORB-SLAM3, DROID-SLAM, HI-SLAM2, Planar-SLAM, and Struct-VIO — without relying on loop closure.
